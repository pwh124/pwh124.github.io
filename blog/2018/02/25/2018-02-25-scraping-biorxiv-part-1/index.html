Scraping bioRxiv Part 1

By Paul Hook | February 25, 2018

Premise

For my first actual blog post, I have decided to start a series about preprints. Awhile ago, an interesting question was brought up by Shannon Ellis in her wonderful blog post “Let there be preprint citations!” (emphasis is mine):

Problem #2: Preprints often become peer-reviewed research. How will we keep track?

Solution #2a (if this occurs during while the manuscript is being drafted): It is up to the author(s) to ensure that their manuscript is as up to date as possible upon submission for publication.*

Solution #2b (if this occurs during while the manuscript is under review): Yup. Still the authors’ responsibility*, but a reminder or tool to ensure accuracy from the publisher would be more than welcome.

Solution #2c (if this occurs post-publication): If the preprint was cited and it later goes through peer review, that does not matter. You cited the preprint. You meant to reference the preprint.

For whatever reason, Shannon posing the question, “How will we keep track?” really piqued my interest.

Now obviously if you are only keeping track of one or two bioRxiv papers that were cited in your paper then you could keep those pages bookmarked and check back when revisions are being made. Outside of the realm of citing preprints in papers, I think having an automated way to keep track of unpublished/published bioRxiv papers could have an everyday, practical use. With Twitter being my main source, I see new bioRxiv papers every day whether it be through authors promoting their own work or one of the bioRxiv bots blasting out recently posted preprints. As the preprints pileup, it becomes more difficult to keep track of what is happening with them, especially since publishing is not a short process.

I also imagained some glorious future where preprints are the norm and you may need to keep an eye on dozens of papers at a time for your manuscript. The task of searching to check to see if those papers were eventually published would no longer be trivial and it would be nice if that task were automated (somewhat).

Goal

Figure out how to automatically check to see if a preprint on bioRxiv is published.

scraping in R

When this idea first came to me, the mysterious world of “HTML scraping” seemed like something that would help. That seemed like a good idea because: A) It doesn’t seem like there are simple/easy ways to access a database within bioRxiv that holds that information and B) Whether or not the paper is published is displayed clearly on each preprint’s page (see below).
